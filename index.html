Background

I began programming at age 9 in BASIC and assembly language. Since age 15, I have been a freelance software engineer for companies like Electronic Arts, L'Oreal, Group SEB/Moulinex, the Paris Airports, and France Electricity, and have been an open source contributor (Ruby on Rails Top 100) and author/maintainer, notably of Ransack, the leading Ruby search engine, but also Kraken Ruby Client, an API client for the Kraken cryptocurrency exchange featured in the Kraken API documentation. I studied Computer Science at Harvey Mudd College in the US, followed by an MBA at INSEAD in France. I also have 2 years of marketing brand management experience in consumer products at L'Oreal and speak English, French, and German.

In March 2019, I began learning to contribute to Bitcoin Core. I started by studying the repository and the social process and specific technical details of contributing to the project. I've been compiling and curating this knowledge at https://github.com/jonatack/bitcoin-development, which is frequently recommended to other developers like in this Bitcoin Magazine September 2019 article and is becoming an educational resource.

It became clear that what is most needed and useful, and paradoxically the least done by new Bitcoin Core contributors, is review and testing of PRs and issues. Roughly 300 pull requests and 700 issues are open at any given moment in Bitcoin Core waiting for experienced review and testing.

I rapidly became one of the most active new contributors and reviewers on Bitcoin Core, as well as on Bitcoin Optech, reviewing each week's newsletter by Dave Harding with John Newbery, Mike Schmidt, Steve Hall, and Adam Jonas, and for the Bitcoin Core PR Review Club, helping John Newbery with improving the codebase and website, hosting meetings, and creating and running the BitcoinCorePRs twitter account.

In early June, Adam Jonas and John Newbery invited me to attend the Chaincode Labs residency seminars. I spent 2 weeks in the Chaincode Labs Manhattan office learning about Bitcoin and meeting core developers and the summer residents. Since then, I have been contributing part-time in the evenings and weekends. Bitcoin Core maintainers and developers frequently ask me for review, testing or a fix as if I were full-time on Bitcoin Core.

Opening pull requests has not been a priority of mine. Yet, by reviewing and understanding what needs to be done and how to make contributions easy to review, I have had 33 commits merged into Bitcoin Core -- more than any other new contributor this year to Bitcoin Core. I am currently #40 of the Bitcoin Core all-time contributors and in the top 10-15 among the currently active contributors. Every Bitcoin Core commit I have made has been merged -- a merge rate of 100%. This shows effectiveness in having improvements merged.

While reviewing, I found and fixed several Bitcoin Core bugs and regressions. Here's the most recent example (I can pick on John since he is incredibly well-respected and a great guy): The regression, and my fix.

Future work in more detail:

I plan to prioritise my time to Bitcoin roughly as follows:

Robustness > Privacy > Features > Scaling > UI.

Thus robustness, censorship resistance, and privacy are my highest priorities.


Goals:

  1. Strengthening Bitcoin Core's decentralisation, censorship resistance and privacy by working to improve the peer-to-peer network privacy and resistance to surveillance and attacks, working with and helping Jonas Schnelli, Suhas Daftuar, Gleb Naumenko, and other people like the Chaincode residents I met this summer, particularly James Chiang and Antoine Riard who have some good ideas.

    Examples:
    • V2 P2P message protocol with opportunistic encryption (BIP 324, BIP 150) by Jonas Schnelli, which was the subject of a presentation I made at Chaincode Labs and a Bitcoin Core PR Review meeting I hosted with Bitcoin Core maintainer Jonas Schnelli.
    • Multiple P2P networks instead of just one.
    • Possibly different IP buckets per peer; each IP would have its own AddrMan table and would confer to ensure they don' have the same peer IPs, to avoid IP poisoning e.g. flooding by attacker peers.
    • Package Relay, separating block relay from transaction relay into 2 networks. Suhas Daftuar is working on this; it will need extensive review and improvements/counter-proposals.
    • Improve our p2p simulation/network testing frameworks to better understand the multi-order effects of proposed improvements.
    • A better framework for systematically thinking about privacy.
    • Better simulations needed for evaluating proposals like Dandelion/++/Lite and others.

  2. Increasing Bitcoin Core's robustness: finding and fixing regressions and bugs, and adding missing test coverage and better testing in under-utilised areas like the P2P network, fuzzing, and property-based testing (which I've helped introduce into Bitcoin Core). Robustness can also mean working on codebase performance, modularity and multiprocess.

  3. The number one bottleneck in Bitcoin Core is experienced review of difficult PRs. I would dedicate more time to deep review of the important PRs that often sit for months without enough review to be merged and have to go through multiple painful rebases, which blocks vital progress and demotivates experienced long-term contributors from working on Bitcoin Core.
    • I would unblock these critical PRs by reviewing them and by coordinating with the maintainers and other contributors to ensure timely review where it is most needed and difficult.
    • Reviewing also means continuing to find and fix regressions and bugs throughout Bitcoin Core and making it more robust.

  4. Improving the Bitcoin Core wallet features, user (GUI) interface, RPC interface, connectivity with hardware wallets, and security.

  5. Investing in developer education and awareness of code review via the Bitcoin Core PR Review Club, and possibly continued review or work with Bitcoin Optech:
    • Develop the Bitcoin Core PR Review Club website and promotion with John Newbery
    • Host Bitcoin Core PR Review Club meetings, like here, and here
    • Develop a backend for the BitcoinCorePRs twitter account to live-tweet Bitcoin Core reviews, high-priority PR updates, and publish monthly rankings of the most active Bitcoin Core reviewers
    • Continue reviewing the Bitcoin Optech newsletters and perhaps other actions to help Dave Harding, John Newbery, and Mike Schmidt.

  6. Regularly presenting the results of the above work at conferences and meetups, providing training, and where feasible, ethically adding value for my sponsor or employer.

  7. Longer term, possibly evolving towards a maintainer role in Bitcoin Core. It fits my profile and experience, there will always be a need, and it takes years to cultivate a maintainer in a project like this.
